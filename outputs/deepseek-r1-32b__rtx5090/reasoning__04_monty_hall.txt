In the Monty Hall problem, you should switch doors. Here's why:

- **Initial Probabilities**: When you first choose a door (e.g., door 1), there is a 1/3 chance it has the car and a 2/3 chance that one of the other two doors does.

- **Host's Action**: The host, who knows what's behind each door, opens another door (e.g., door 3) to reveal a goat. This action doesn't change the initial probabilities but provides information.

- **Updated Probabilities**: Since the host always reveals a goat, the 2/3 probability that the car was behind one of the other two doors now shifts entirely to the remaining unopened door (door 2). Thus, switching gives you a 2/3 chance of winning, while staying with your original choice keeps it at 1/3.

**Conclusion**: Switching doubles your chances of winning from 1/3 to 2/3 because the host's action reveals information that changes the probability distribution.
