The proof that claims 2 = 1 is a classic example of a mathematical fallacy. The error occurs in **Step 4**, where both sides of the equation are divided by **(a - b)**. Here's the breakdown:

1. **Initial Setup**:  
   Let $ a = b $.  
   Multiply both sides by $ a $: $ a^2 = ab $.  
   Subtract $ b^2 $: $ a^2 - b^2 = ab - b^2 $.  
   Factor both sides: $ (a + b)(a - b) = b(a - b) $.  

2. **Critical Error**:  
   The next step divides both sides by $ (a - b) $:  
   $$
   \frac{(a + b)(a - b)}{a - b} = \frac{b(a - b)}{a - b}.
   $$  
   However, since $ a = b $, $ (a - b) = 0 $. **Division by zero is undefined** in mathematics, making this step invalid.  

3. **Consequences**:  
   The rest of the proof relies on this invalid operation. Simplifying the equation gives $ a + b = b $, which leads to $ 2b = b $ and $ 2 = 1 $. These conclusions are nonsensical because they depend on the earlier division by zero.  

**Why This Is Invalid**:  
Division by zero is undefined because it violates the fundamental properties of arithmetic. Specifically, if $ x = 0 $, there is no number $ y $ such that $ 0 \cdot y = x $ (unless $ x = 0 $, but even then, $ y $ is not uniquely defined). By dividing by $ (a - b) = 0 $, the proof introduces an undefined operation, rendering all subsequent steps meaningless.  

**Conclusion**:  
The proof is flawed because it divides by $ (a - b) $, which equals zero. This invalid step allows the derivation of a false conclusion (2 = 1). The key takeaway is to always verify that denominators are non-zero in algebraic manipulations.  

**Answer**:  
The proof goes wrong in the step where both sides are divided by $ (a - b) $. Since $ a = b $, $ (a - b) = 0 $, and division by zero is undefined. This invalid operation leads to the false conclusion.
