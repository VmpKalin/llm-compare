In the Monty Hall problem, you should switch doors. If you stay with your initial choice (door 1), you have a **1/3 chance of winning** the car, as your initial pick had a 1/3 probability of being correct. However, if you switch to the remaining unopened door (door 2), your probability of winning increases to **2/3**. This is because the host’s action of revealing a goat (door 3) provides additional information: if the car was initially behind door 2 or 3 (which occurs 2/3 of the time), the host effectively eliminates the wrong door, leaving the car behind the unchosen door. Switching capitalizes on this 2/3 likelihood, making it the optimal strategy.
